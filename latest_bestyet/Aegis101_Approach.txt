1. SYSTEM ARCHITECTURE & PIPELINE APPROACH
================================================================================
Our approach treats the gridlock problem as a highly skewed, spatio-temporal regression task. Rather than relying on leaky global averages or naive time-series lags that fail due to the limited 2-day dataset, we engineered a rigorous, structurally diverse Gradient Boosting ensemble optimized directly for the R² metric.

The pipeline executes sequentially in a single Jupyter Notebook (Gridlock_Submission.ipynb):
1. Data Ingestion & Leakage Prevention: We aggressively purge 'Day 49' from the training data. This prevents future temporal leakage and forces the models to genuinely learn spatial generalization rather than memorizing a "lookup table."
2. Feature Engineering: Extraction of spatial density, coordinate geometry, and explicit categorical infrastructure crosses.
3. K-Fold Target Encoding: Transforming meaningless geohash strings into pure mathematical risk weights using a strict Out-Of-Fold mapping loop.
4. Intra-Day Time-Series CV: We validate locally using a 5-Fold Expanding Window Time-Series Split on Day 48 to ensure chronological integrity.
5. Direct R² Stacking (Nelder-Mead): Base models are blended using a Scipy Optimizer that mathematically hunts for fractional weights to strictly maximize R², replacing standard MSE-based Ridge regressors.

================================================================================
2. FEATURE ENGINEERING DETAILS
================================================================================
We engineered explicit features to help the trees quickly separate the signals:

A. Spatial & Topological Features:
- Coordinate Geometry: Transformed 'lat' and 'lon' into 3D Cartesian coordinates (x, y, z) to give the models an accurate representation of physical distance.
- Spatial Density (KNN): Used a KNeighborsRegressor on the coordinates to estimate the clustering density of high-demand traffic hubs.
- K-Fold Geohash Encoding: Replaced 'geohash' with its historical mean demand using an isolated 5-Fold split. This converts high-cardinality strings into a continuous risk-weight without causing local target leakage.

B. Temporal Features:
- Cyclical Time: Applied sine and cosine transformations to 'hour' and 'minute' so the trees understand that 23:45 is chronologically adjacent to 00:00.
- Peak Flags: Extracted Boolean features for 'is_morning_peak' and 'is_evening_peak'.

C. Contextual Infrastructure Crosses (Non-Linear Shortcuts):
To prevent the trees from wasting depth on complex interactions, we explicitly crossed critical categories:
- 'weather_x_peak': E.g., Raining + Rush Hour.
- 'capacity_risk': Number of Lanes + Large Vehicles Allowed.
- 'road_weather': Road Type + Weather severity.

D. Imputation Strategy:
- Temperature was imputed using Geohash-grouped spatial means rather than a flat global median, preserving localized micro-climate data which proved to be a high-importance signal.

================================================================================
3. MODELS & TOOLS USED
================================================================================
Our architecture utilizes a mathematically stacked ensemble of the three most powerful gradient boosting frameworks:

1. LightGBM (lgb): Fast, leaf-wise tree growth. Excellent at handling the high-cardinality spatial features and finding deep interactions.
2. CatBoost (cb): Unmatched handling of categorical infrastructure crosses without heavy preprocessing. Extremely robust to overfitting.
3. XGBoost (xgb): Provides heavy depth-wise regularization to control the extreme variance in the skewed demand targets.

Tools / Libraries:
- Python (Pandas, NumPy, Scikit-Learn)
- Boosting Libraries (lightgbm, catboost, xgboost)
- Optuna: Used offline for hyperparameter tuning.
- Scipy (scipy.optimize.minimize): Used to deploy the Nelder-Mead algorithm for Direct R² Optimization during the final stacking phase.
